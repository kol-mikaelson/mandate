The narrow lanes of Mylapore have witnessed centuries of change — from colonial restructuring to post-independence bureaucratic reordering. But the current threat to its built heritage is perhaps the most insidious: it arrives not in the form of demolition orders but as individual building permissions, granted quietly, accumulating into irreversible transformation.

Over the past eighteen months, at least fourteen heritage structures on the Conservation Register have been flagged for redevelopment under the revised Chennai Metropolitan Development Authority zoning regulations.

> "We are not against housing. We are against the pretence that destroying a hundred-year-old structure and replacing it with a sixteen-storey tower constitutes a housing solution."

## The Regulatory Gap

Chennai's heritage protection framework was designed in an era before the current FSI liberalisation. The Conservation Register, maintained by the CMDA, lists over 600 structures across the city. But listing does not prevent demolition.

The question is not whether Chennai can afford to preserve its heritage. It is whether the city has the political will to insist that its planning instruments mean something.
